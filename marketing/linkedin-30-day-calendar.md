## WEEK 1: Establish Credibility & Hook

**Day 1 — The Origin Story**
> Most AI prompt advice is useless.
> 
> I spent 6 months testing prompts with professionals — real estate agents, product managers, mechanics, financial advisors.
> 
> 90% of what I read online produces generic garbage. The remaining 10% is why I built Workshift.
> 
> The difference between a good prompt and a great one isn't complexity. It's specificity. Tell the AI who you are, what the output is for, and what "good" looks like to you.
> 
> That's the whole system. Everything else is just filling in the blanks.
> 
> What's the worst AI output you've ever gotten? I'll tell you what went wrong.

**Day 2 — The Bracket Method (Educational)**
> Here's the single most useful thing I can teach you about AI prompts.
> 
> Stop writing this: "Write me an email to a client."
> 
> Write this instead: "Write a follow-up email to [CLIENT NAME], a [ROLE] at [COMPANY], who [SPECIFIC CONTEXT]. The goal is [OUTCOME]. Tone: [PROFESSIONAL/WARM/DIRECT]."
> 
> Brackets are placeholders. They force you to give the AI enough context to do its job.
> 
> This single habit will improve your AI output more than any other tip you'll find.
> 
> Try it today and tell me if it changes anything.

**Day 3 — Profession-Specific Hook**
> I asked 50 real estate agents what they spend the most time writing.
> 
> The answers: MLS descriptions, client follow-up emails, and social media posts.
> 
> That's roughly 5–8 hours a week. Per agent.
> 
> AI can draft all three in under 10 minutes total — if you know how to ask.
> 
> The agents who've figured this out aren't just saving time. They're producing more consistent, higher-quality communication than they ever did manually.
> 
> If you're in real estate and want the exact prompts I use, reply "real estate" and I'll send them.

**Day 4 — Myth Bust**
> "AI will make everyone sound the same."
> 
> This is the #1 objection I hear from professionals who haven't tried it properly.
> 
> It's also wrong.
> 
> Generic AI output is the result of generic prompts. When you tell AI your voice, your audience, your goals, and your constraints — it sounds like you. A better version of you who never has a bad writing day.
> 
> The homogenization problem is a prompting problem. It's entirely solvable.

**Day 5 — Quick Win (Shareable)**
> 5 AI prompts that actually work for professionals:
> 
> 1. "Rewrite this email to sound more direct without being rude: [PASTE EMAIL]"
> 2. "Turn these bullet points into a clear executive summary: [PASTE BULLETS]"
> 3. "Write 3 subject line options for an email about [TOPIC]. Goal: [OUTCOME]"
> 4. "I need to say no to this request. Write a professional response that keeps the relationship: [CONTEXT]"
> 5. "Create a one-page brief on [TOPIC] for someone who has 5 minutes to read it"
> 
> Save this. Use it this week.

**Day 6 — For Product Managers**
> Product managers write more than most people realize.
> 
> PRDs. Stakeholder updates. User stories. Roadmap narratives. Meeting summaries. Escalation emails.

> 
> The PMs I've worked with who use AI well ship 30–40% more documentation without burning out.
>

**Day 7 — Counterintuitive Take**
> The biggest AI mistake isn't trusting it too much.
> 
> It's using it for the wrong things.
> 
> AI is terrible at: knowing your specific context, making judgment calls, building relationships.
> 
> AI is excellent at: first drafts, reformatting, explaining things clearly, producing 10 versions of something quickly.
> 
> The professionals getting the most from AI aren't the ones who use it for everything. They're the ones who've figured out exactly where it saves them an hour and where it wastes one.

**Day 8 — For Trades Professionals**

> Or how a contractor can turn a napkin scope into a professional proposal in 5 minutes.
> 
> Or how a restaurant owner can respond to a 1-star review without making it worse.
> 
> AI isn't just for knowledge workers. It's for anyone who has to communicate professionally.

## WEEK 3: Objection Handling & Education

**Day 11 — "I tried AI and it didn't work"**
> The most common reason AI doesn't work for professionals: they tried it once with a bad prompt, got a mediocre result, and gave up.
> 
> That's like trying to use a power drill with no bit, deciding it doesn't work, and going back to a hammer.
> 
> The tool is fine. The setup needs work.
> 
> If you've tried ChatGPT or Claude and felt like it wasn't worth your time — tell me what you asked it. I'll show you what I'd ask instead.

**Day 12 — The Real ROI of AI**
> People focus on time savings. That's real, but it's not the whole story.
> 
> The bigger gain is consistency.
> 
> A real estate agent who prompts well writes the same quality MLS description at 5pm on Friday as they do on a fresh Tuesday morning.
> 
> A financial advisor who prompts well sends the same quality client email whether they're energized or exhausted.
> 
> AI doesn't just save time. It removes your worst days.

**Day 13 — For HR & Recruiters**
> Job descriptions are the most important thing recruiters write.
> 
> They determine who applies, how good those candidates are, and whether the best people even consider you.
> 
> Most job descriptions are terrible. Laundry lists of requirements, vague role descriptions, and "competitive salary."
> 
> AI won't fix a bad brief. But given a good brief, it will write a job description in 3 minutes that would have taken an hour — and that actually sounds like a place people want to work.

**Day 14 — Be Honest / Build Trust**
> Something I want to be upfront about with Workshift:
> 
> The prompts won't make bad writing good if you don't review them.
> 
> AI drafts fast. You still need to edit. The goal isn't to publish the first output — it's to start from something good instead of starting from nothing.
> 
> Every professional who uses these prompts well treats AI like a first draft, not a finished product.
> 
> That's the right mindset. If someone tells you otherwise, they're selling something.

**Day 15 — Weekend Value Post**
> The 80/20 of professional AI use:
> 
> 80% of the value comes from 20% of use cases. For most knowledge workers, that 20% is:
> 
> — First drafts of routine communications (emails, updates, reports)
> — Reformatting and restructuring existing content
> — Generating options quickly (5 subject lines, 3 headlines, 4 angles)
> — Explaining complex things in simpler language
> 
> Master those four. Everything else is gravy.

**Day 17 — Show Don't Tell**
> Here's a prompt I use constantly:
> 
> "I need to write [DOCUMENT TYPE] for [AUDIENCE]. The context is [BRIEF DESCRIPTION]. Key points to cover: [LIST]. Tone: [DESCRIBE]. Write a first draft I can edit."
> 
> That's it. That structure works for emails, reports, proposals, status updates, performance reviews, and client messages.
> 
> The brackets are the secret. Fill them in completely and the output is usually 80% of the way there.

**Day 18 — For Sales Reps**
> Cold outreach is a volume game with terrible economics — unless you personalize.
> 
> The problem: real personalization takes 15 minutes per prospect. At scale, it's impossible.
> 
> AI changes that math. 
> 
> Give it 3 facts about a prospect and a clear value proposition and it'll write a genuinely personalized opening in 30 seconds.
>

**Day 19 — Educational: Context vs Prompt**
> There's a difference between prompting and context engineering.
> 
> Prompting: telling AI what to write.
> Context engineering: telling AI who you are, who you're writing for, what "good" looks like, and what to avoid.
> 
> Most people only do the first. The ones who do both consistently get dramatically better results.
> 
> Context engineering isn't complicated. It's just answering: "What would I tell a smart new hire before asking them to write this?"
